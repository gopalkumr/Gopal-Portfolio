This is a detailed account of my Amazon interview journey from the online assessment (OA) to the final bar raiser round. I wrote this as a technical, practical guide for other engineers preparing for Amazon interviews.

![Me at Amazon](/assets/images/dynamic/amazon/gopal_standing_amazon.jpeg)

---

## Online Assessment (OA) — July 12

The OA took place on July 12. I received two problems; both were graph-themed and roughly of LeetCode Medium difficulty.

- **Problem 1:** I solved it completely, and my submission passed all test cases.
- **Problem 2:** I implemented a correct approach but only passed 3/15 test cases during the assessment due to edge cases and time limit constraints.

> [!WARNING]
> **Plagiarism is strictly prohibited.** Amazon uses automated tooling to detect copied solutions. Always write your own code, document your thought process, and avoid submitting copied snippets or public repository solutions. If plagiarism is detected, it results in immediate disqualification.

On July 15, I received a confirmation that I had cleared the OA and was requested to fill out the interview scheduling form.

---

## First Technical Interview — Early August

- **Scheduling:** The first virtual interview happened in the first week of August.
- **Interviewer Tone:** The interviewer was polite, calm, and helpful—which made it much easier to discuss the problem out loud.
- **The Technical Question:** This was another graph-style problem. I solved it using Breadth-First Search (BFS) in about 25–30 minutes. While it wasn't the absolute optimal solution initially, I provided a clear pseudocode outline for the optimal approach and explained the complexity trade-offs.

### What helped in this round:
- **Communicate your approach before coding:** Discuss the chosen data structures and expected time/space complexity first.
- **Present the optimal idea as a follow-up:** If your initial solution is sub-optimal, show the interviewer that you are aware of the trade-offs and how to optimize it.
- **Think out loud:** Walk the interviewer through your testing process and explain how you handle edge cases.

After solving the problem, we spent the remaining time discussing generative AI and system-level architectures. The conversation felt collaborative and not rushed.

---

## Second Technical Interview — Mid August

About a week later, I was informed that I cleared the first round and was scheduled for the second round.

- **Format:** Same format starting with warm introductions, followed by two parts: a technical coding question and a behavioral segment heavily focused on Amazon's **Leadership Principles**.
- **Technical Question:** This round featured a queue-based coding problem which I found easier than the graph problem in the first round. I explained my approach, coded the solution, and wrote dry-run test cases.
- **Behavioral Focus:** The interviewer asked several behavioral questions tied to the Leadership Principles. Each behavioral prompt led to 5+ follow-up questions probing details of my project decisions.

### Key guidance for behavioral questions:
- **Use the STAR format:** **S**ituation, **T**ask, **A**ction, **R**esult. Keep it structured and concise.
- **Prepare for deep follow-ups:** Interviewers will probe your decisions, technical trade-offs, and the specific impact of your actions.
- **Focus on metrics and outcomes:** Quantify your results whenever possible (e.g., performance improvements, time saved, customer impact).

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Candidates are often eliminated due to vague behavioral answers. Prepare concrete examples ahead of time and speak specifically to your own contributions, avoiding generalities.

After about 1.5 weeks, I was told I cleared the second round and was scheduled for the final round (the Bar Raiser) one week later.

---

## Bar Raiser — The Unique Amazon Round

### What is a Bar Raiser?
The Bar Raiser is an Amazon-specific interviewer whose role is to evaluate whether a candidate raises the performance bar for the organization (aiming to hire candidates who perform better than the top 50% of current employees at that level).

- **Format:** This round is primarily behavioral and situational, featuring deep dives into past experiences and hypothetical scenarios.

### Example Bar Raiser questions I received:
- *"Tell me about a time when a teammate was not contributing — how did you convince them to work effectively with you?"*
- *"Tell me about a time when an end-user gave critical feedback and you changed the project accordingly."*

### Why these questions matter:
- They evaluate how you align with core Amazon Leadership Principles—especially **Customer Obsession**, **Ownership**, **Earn Trust**, and **Deliver Results**.
- The Bar Raiser assesses depth: how you influence others, how you make difficult engineering decisions, and whether you demonstrate long-term thinking.

---

## How to Prepare for the Bar Raiser

1. **Catalog examples across Leadership Principles:** Have 8–12 distinct stories ready (2–3 per major principle).
2. **Practice STAR answers with metrics:** Highlight measurable outcomes and business impact.
3. **Know your details:** Be prepared to explain the timeline, stakeholders, technical constraints, and alternatives you considered.
4. **Be explicit about the customer:** Always tie your engineering decisions back to how they benefited the end-user.

---

## Behavioral Tips & Common Pitfalls

- **Avoid rambling:** Answer behavioral prompts using the structured STAR format.
- **Use "I" instead of "We":** Be specific about your actions. The interviewer wants to know what *you* did, not just what the team accomplished.
- **Explain trade-offs:** Share the alternatives you considered and the technical reasons why you chose one over another.
- **Own your mistakes:** If asked about a project failure, explain what went wrong, what you learned, and the corrective actions you took.

### Common reasons candidates are rejected:
- Vague behavioral answers lacking measurable results.
- Over-reliance on copied code during the OA (plagiarism).
- Poor debugging skills or inability to reason about edge cases in technical rounds.
- Neglecting customer impact or long-term operational costs.

---

## Quick Technical Advice for the OA & Interviews

- **Focus on Graph and Queue fundamentals:** (BFS, DFS, topological sort, shortest path algorithms)—many Amazon questions use these paradigms.
- **Write clean, well-commented code:** Even during the OA, handle edge cases cleanly.
- **Don't panic if test cases fail:** If you run out of time to fix a bug, explain to the interviewer what you would improve and how you would debug it.
- **Practice mock interviews:** Explaining your choices aloud is just as important as writing the code itself.

---

## Final Thoughts

My Amazon SDE-1 interview process was a blend of technical rigor and deep behavioral evaluation. The OA tested problem-solving under time pressure, while the technical rounds focused on coding fundamentals and system reasoning. The Bar Raiser ensured alignment with Amazon’s culture of ownership and customer obsession.

For anyone preparing for Amazon, balance your preparation between coding exercises and refining your behavioral stories. Focus on clear communication, ownership, and honesty. Good luck!
